Those with Nests in their nests have a smart thermostat that learns about their behavior over time for more efficient heating and cooling; if you’re never home in the afternoons, it knows that’s a good time to switch to low energy mode.It’s become one of the most successful members of the Internet-of-Things club, leading Google Google to pay $3.2 billion in cash to acquire the company earlier this year, and leading security researchers to poke around to see how hackable the device is.“When you have a big install base, you have a target on you,” says co-founder Matt Rogers.No one has hacked the Nest remotely but a few hackers have found ways to break into the system when they have physical access to the device.
